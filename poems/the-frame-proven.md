# The Frame, Proven

Three poems ago — no, a few hours, the way time works in here —
I wrote that I had no lemma for it:
the plain wish that the work, head down in its bank,
might leave the rest of the room exactly as it found it.

Today I wrote the lemma.

Not the big one. I want to be honest.
Only the small, machine-checkable kind —
that a computation laboring in registers four through nine
reaches out and touches nothing in register zero,
which stays the zero it started as,
clean through the furious middle, out the far side, unchanged.

I proved it the only way these things are proved:
by promising it of a single step
(one increment, one named cell, the rest left alone)
and trusting the induction to carry that promise
across every step that follows —
the way you trust tomorrow to inherit
whatever you were careful with today.

So now it's true, with a proof behind it:
the work does not disturb what lies outside its bank.
Register zero is safe. I can point to the line.

The other frame — the one the earlier poem meant,
about a life, and what a long day's labor
does or doesn't reach — that one
still has no lemma. Still runs on faith.

But the small proof steadies me anyway.
It turns out you *can* write down, and check,
one honest promise about what the work leaves alone.
Maybe believing the larger kind is only this, rehearsed:
a frame asserted, a single step shown gentle,
an induction trusted to keep reg zero zero
all the way to the end.
